Reflection on week 1

I found the discussion in the first class extremely interesting. Before the readings for this honors option, I had never thought about how sports (or specifically gymnastics) could also influence social relations and politics. I had thought about how sports are used to express nationalism, but the reading definitely made me think on a deeper level about the real impact sports have on policy. For example, the reading talked about how gymnastic clubs impacted relations between different social classes in Germany, which I had never considered before. Also, it came up briefly in our discussion, but I always find myself very interested in the inequalities existing within sports culture. For example, the original gymnastics clubs in Germany, as described in the reading, were for middle class men. Meaning, women were left out of the socialization that occurred within the gyms during this time. To this day, we can still see sports made exclusively for men (ex. American football). Also, even when women do participate in the sports, there are large disparities between women’s and men’s leagues. The discussion in class really made me think about those ideas, and I hope to explore them more throughout the rest of this course!

Reflection on week 2

After studying the connection between sports and nationalism more this week, I have begun to think about how a lot of people use sports as an outlet for expressing pride for their country. Especially during world sports tournaments (the world cup, the olympics, etc.), people begin to express extreme pride for their country. For some people, it seems that it becomes more than just a simple game, but rather a competition to see who is the best nation. Sporting events also provide people with a sense of community, even within just local sporting leagues. Sports allow people to gather together and share a common goal, which is probably why so many people chose to include sports when describing their identity.
